RE: Science Correspondent, Print Desk

Dear Hiring Editor,

I am writing to apply for the Science Correspondent position on your print desk, and I will
not waste the ink pretending I am a calm and balanced applicant about it. I want this job the
way a moth wants the porch light.

There is a notion abroad – you have heard it, we all have – that print is dying. People say it
the way they report the weather, as a settled fact requiring no further thought. I have made it
my professional habit to require further thought. The truth, as near as I can establish it, is
that print is not dying so much as being repeatedly pronounced dead by people who would rather
not pay for it. Those are different conditions, and the difference is the whole of my career.

My qualifications are these: five years, several hundred thousand words, and a stubbornness
that my editors have learned to file under "asset" rather than "problem." At The Gutenberg
Dispatch I write a weekly column on the science of aging, which has taught me a useful trick –
how to keep a thing alive at the ends, where it frays first. I have applied the lesson to the
science section's page count with rather more success than the budget meetings predicted.

What I would bring to your desk is plain enough: I can take a paper so dense it could anchor a
boat and hand your readers back something they will actually finish, remember, and possibly
clip and mail to a relative. I have never confused being accurate with being unreadable, nor
being readable with being wrong. I check the second source. I meet the deadline. I assume the
reader is smart and busy, which is the only assumption that has ever served me.

I would be glad to come in and prove all this in person, where I am told I am marginally more
persuasive than on paper – though I have staked my whole living on paper being persuasive
enough.

With earnest regard and a freshly sharpened pencil,
Tila Mer
