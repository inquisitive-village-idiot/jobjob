# STAR: The Budget Meeting
Competencies: Leadership, Communication

**Situation.** The paper's third-quarter numbers came in soft, and management did what
management does when numbers come in soft: it went looking for a limb to saw off. The science
section – two pages, one full-time correspondent, no advertising to speak of – was the limb. I
was given fifteen minutes on the agenda, which I understood to mean I was being invited to my
own funeral and asked to bring the eulogy.

**Task.** I needed to keep the section in print, and I needed the rest of the newsroom behind
me when I did it – a junior reporter cannot win that room alone. So before the meeting was ever
the meeting, I had to make the case to the people who'd be cut alongside me, and give them a
reason to stand up too.

**Action.** I spent the week before the meeting talking to every desk that touched our coverage
– the city reporter who leaned on me for the water-quality numbers, the features editor whose
health pieces I fact-checked. Not to lobby them, exactly, but to remind them out loud how much
of their own work ran through the science desk. By the time I stood up, three of them had
already said a quiet word to the editor. Then I brought the mail: eleven months of reader
letters, the physical ones, addressed by hand. I read three aloud and put the stack on the
table, because a pile of letters is an argument you cannot rebut with a spreadsheet.

**Result.** The section stayed, and the following year I talked them into a half-page
expansion, on the grounds that you do not grow a readership by feeding it less. The lesson I
kept: leadership, for someone with no title to speak of, is mostly the work you do before the
room – making sure you walk in already not alone.
