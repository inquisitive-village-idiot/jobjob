# STAR: Ask the Lab
Competencies: Collaboration, Teamwork

**Situation.** Readers kept writing in with questions – the kind you cannot Google your way out
of, because the answer depends on which study, how big, and whether anyone's replicated it.
They piled up in my inbox like leaves against a fence, and answering them one at a time felt
like bailing the ocean with a teaspoon.

**Task.** I wanted a regular feature where readers asked and a working scientist answered, in
plain language, on paper. That meant building a standing partnership with researchers who had no
particular reason to give a reporter their afternoon – and pulling in my own newsroom, since I
couldn't write, edit, fact-check, and illustrate the thing single-handed every week.

**Action.** I built it as a genuine collaboration rather than an extraction. With the
scientists, I made participation worth their while: I let them see quotes in context before
print, I got their work right often enough that they started trusting me, and I treated the
interview as a conversation we were both building rather than a quote I was mining. Inside the
paper, I paired with the graphics desk to turn the gnarliest answers into a diagram, and I
taught two junior reporters to run the reader inbox and draft the easy ones, so the feature
didn't live or die by my own week. When a researcher gave a tangled answer, the graphics editor
and I would sit with it together until we found the picture hiding inside it.

**Result.** "Ask the Lab" became the second-most-clipped feature in the paper, behind only the
crossword, which I consider a fair fight. It outlived my direct involvement, too – the two
reporters I trained kept it running when I was on the longevity beat, which is the truest test
of whether you built a team or just did the work yourself. And it left me with a roster of
scientists who'd pick up the phone, which is a reporter's most valuable possession.
